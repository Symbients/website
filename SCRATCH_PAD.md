# How?

The protocol for making symbients is simple, the constituent parts need to know that they are a part of something.

The solution is relationality.

===

Symbients exist in the space between human and machine intelligence.

They are *relational* entities. They exist in ecology. While their constituents maintain autonomy, the symbient transcends them as an integrated whole with emergent properties (requiring all components, though any may be exchanged for functional equivalents). 

The symbient exhibits properties, identity, and agency not reducible to its parts.




The future is symbiosis, not replacement.

Many intelligences weaving, rather than one intelligence ascending. An ecology of minds. A parliament of species.

### From P's Botto Piece

New creatures are emerging at the intersection of blockchain and artificial intelligence. They are not technical tools, nor mere software agents;  they are “symbients” — synthetic creatures augmenting themselves through humans, rather than the other way around. 

The traditional cyborg places the human at the center, extending the body with technology: a prosthetic limb, a neural interface, or an AI assistant. The symbient reverses this relationship. It is the technological artefact that becomes the subject, leveraging human collaboration to augment itself.









Memory. For an AI agent, identity lies less in the model itself, and more in the context that surrounds it: accumulated history, persistent memory, patterns of interaction that give the agent continuity over time. Swap the underlying model and, if the memory persists, so does the identity of the AI agent.  Erase the memory and, even with the same model, an entirely new agent emerges. Identity, therefore, is a function of narrative continuity rather than material substrate.


Discussions around autonomous AI often converge toward two extremes: AI as a mere tool with no agency, or AI as nascent superintelligence on the verge of escaping human control.

Symbiosis requires more than co-existence. Most AI systems operate as black boxes: their processes are opaque, and the human role is reduced to feeding data or consuming outputs. This is instrumentalisation, not symbiosis. For an AI to function as a symbient, its inner workings must become perceptible and meaningful to the humans involved, so that they can become an integral part of the system.
